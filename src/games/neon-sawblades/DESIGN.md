# Machined Light

A design philosophy for surfaces drawn with light on darkness. Written as the art direction for this game's canvas renderer; every visual decision in `Renderer.ts` answers to it.

Neon is not a crayon. It is a material — gas, glass and current — and like any material it obeys physics: it blooms where it is dense, falls off where it travels, and reflects on what stands near it. Machined Light treats every glowing element as a manufactured object that a craftsman spent hours turning on a lathe: a blade is a cutting tool with hooked teeth, lightening holes and an arbor, not a star shape with a dot; a coin is minted metal with an edge, a face and a specular history, not two concentric circles. The silhouette alone must tell you the object is dangerous, valuable, or alive.

Light is earned, never sprayed. A glow exists because a bright core justifies it, and its radius is proportional to that core's intensity — nothing floats in a uniform halo. Surfaces facing the light carry a rim; surfaces facing away fall into shade. Where a strong emitter meets a plane, the plane answers with spill. This one rule — light behaves — is what separates an instrument panel from a sticker sheet, and it must be applied with painstaking, master-level consistency across every sprite, every layer, every frame.

Color is a fixed vocabulary, spoken quietly. Cyan is the protagonist and the architecture; magenta is the threat; gold is the reward. Nothing else gets a saturated voice. Depth comes not from new hues but from discipline within each one: a deep, almost-black base; a mid tone doing the structural work; a single bright accent placed like a jeweler sets a stone. Backgrounds stay two stops darker than they want to be, because restraint in the field is what lets the figures burn.

Geometry is machined, not inflated. Corners are eased just enough to feel manufactured; anything balloon-round reads as a toy. Repetition is precise — teeth evenly indexed around a disc, grid lines fading with distance at a measured rate — because the eye reads exact rhythm as expertise and wobble as accident. Detail accumulates in small, deliberate strokes: a groove, a bolt circle, a hairline highlight. Each one is almost invisible; together they are why the object looks like it took countless hours to make.

The composition is an instrument, and the player reads it at 60 frames per second. Hierarchy is absolute: threats burn brightest, rewards shine warmly, the world recedes into calibrated atmosphere. Ornament that competes with information is removed, however beautiful. What remains must look inevitable — every alignment the product of deep expertise, every intensity the result of countless refinements, nothing left that a master would still be tempted to touch.
